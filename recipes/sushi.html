Sushi

Description

Sushi is a traditional Japanese dish that centres on vinegared rice, often paired with seafood, vegetables, or egg. While many people associate sushi with raw fish, the key ingredient is actually the rice, seasoned with a subtle mix of vinegar, sugar, and salt. The dish takes many forms—nigiri (a slice of fish pressed over a small bed of rice), maki (rolls wrapped in seaweed), and chirashi (scattered toppings over a bowl of rice), to name a few. Sushi highlights balance and simplicity, showcasing the natural flavours of fresh ingredients rather than masking them with heavy seasoning.
Over the years, sushi has grown from a Japanese delicacy into a global culinary phenomenon. Outside Japan, sushi has been adapted to local palates with creations like the California roll, which often substitutes avocado and imitation crab for more traditional ingredients. High-end sushi restaurants may serve omakase, a chef’s choice tasting menu, where the emphasis is on seasonality and artistry. Whether enjoyed as an affordable lunch at a conveyor-belt restaurant or as a carefully crafted fine-dining experience, sushi represents both Japan’s culinary heritage and the world’s fascination with fresh, elegant food.

Ingredients

  • 200 g sushi-grade fish (tuna, salmon, or shrimp)
  • 250 g sushi rice (short-grain, cooked)
  • 3 tbsp rice vinegar
  • 1 tbsp sugar
  • 1/2 tsp salt
  • Wasabi (optional)

Steps

  1. Cook sushi rice, then season with vinegar, sugar, and salt. Let it cool slightly.
  2. Slice fish into thin, even pieces about 6 cm long.
  3. Wet your hands, shape rice into small ovals.
  4. Place a dab of wasabi on the fish, lay it on top of the rice, press gently.
  5. Serve with soy sauce and pickled ginger.

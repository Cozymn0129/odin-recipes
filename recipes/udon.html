Udon

Description

Udon is a type of thick, chewy Japanese noodle made from wheat flour, water, and salt, often served in a hot broth or chilled with dipping sauce. Its simplicity allows it to take on a wide variety of flavours, from the gentle savouriness of a clear dashi broth topped with scallions, to richer variations like curry udon, where the noodles are bathed in a spiced sauce. The texture of udon is central to its appeal—soft yet pleasantly firm, making it hearty and satisfying. Classic toppings include tempura, thinly sliced beef, or a raw egg, but the noodles themselves are versatile enough to stand as the star of the dish.
Across Japan, regional styles of udon reflect local tastes and traditions. In Kagawa Prefecture, known as the “Udon Prefecture,” sanuki udon is especially famous for its firm, springy bite, while in Kansai, the broth tends to be lighter and more delicate. Beyond Japan, udon has also gained popularity internationally, often appearing in Japanese restaurants alongside ramen and soba. Whether enjoyed as a comforting bowl on a cold day or a chilled dish in summer, udon is cherished for its adaptability and its role as a staple of Japanese comfort food.

Ingredients

  • 2 servings udon noodles
  • 600 ml dashi stock (kombu + bonito flakes, or instant powder)
  • 2 tbsp soy sauce
  • 2 tbsp mirin
  • 1 tsp sugar
  • sliced spring onion
  • tempura bits (tenkasu)
  • kamaboko (fish cake)
  • seaweed

Steps

  1. Boil udon noodles according to package, then rinse lightly and drain.
  2. In a saucepan, combine dashi, soy sauce, mirin, and sugar; bring to a gentle simmer.
  3. Place noodles in bowls, pour hot broth over them.
  4. Top with spring onion, fish cake, nori, or even tempura for extra flair.
